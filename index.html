Democracy and the Environment
Annual Review of Political Science (Forthcoming). With Kathryn Baragwanath.
Download PDF

We survey research on the relationship between democracy and the environment. We show scholars find, at best, a weakly positive correlation between democracy and environment, with little support for the proposed Democratic Environmental Kuznets Curve, a finding we confirm with new data.

Air pollution could be reduced by incentivizing local government officials to control crop burning
Nature: Policy Brief (2025). With Gemma Dipoppa.
Download PDF

Crop-residue burning in South Asia decreases by up to 14.5% when smoke from fires would affect bureaucrats’ home districts instead of neighbouring ones. Bureaucratic action to punish farmers deters future fires by a further 13%. Thus, using administrator incentives to control crop burning could guide policy towards improving air quality and reducing infant and child mortality.

Bureaucrat Incentives Reduce Crop Burning and Child Mortality in South Asia
Nature (2024). With Gemma Dipoppa.
Download PDF

Air pollution in South Asia is one of the largest health emergencies on the planet. We show that crop burning responds to bureaucrats’ incentives: fires increase by 15% when wind is most likely to direct pollution to neighboring jurisdictions and decreases by 14.5% when it pollutes their own.

Representation and Forest Conservation: Evidence from India’s Scheduled Areas
American Political Science Review (2024). With Apoorva Lal and Benjamin Pasquale.
Download PDF

How does political representation affect conservation? We study a 1996 law that created local government councils with mandated representation for India's Scheduled Tribes. We find that the dramatic increase in ST representation led to a substantial increase in tree cover and a reduction in deforestation.

Political Responsiveness, Information Provision, and Capacity Gaps
Comparative Political Studies (Forthcoming). With Miriam Golden and Luke Sonnet.
Download PDF

To investigate whether lack of information is one reason politicians may be unresponsive to voter preferences, we conduct a randomized control trial with senior politicians in Pakistan. Our findings reveal that even eager and informed politicians may be unable to respond to citizen preferences due to institutional capacity constraints.

Can Party Elites Shape the Rank-and-File? Evidence from a Recruitment Experiment in India
American Political Science Review (2025). With Durgesh Pathak, Sarah Thompson, and Aliz Toth.
Download PDF

We analyze a field experiment conducted by an Indian party that randomized recruitment messages. Contrary to concerns that parties can only attract a few poor-quality volunteers, we show that elite efforts can shape the rank-and-file. Strategies that signal gender inclusiveness have a lasting impact.

Political Economy of Development

Publications

“Political Responsiveness, Information Provision, and Capacity Gaps” (forthcoming)
Comparative Political Studies. With Miriam Golden and Luke Sonnet.
PDF Cite

We investigate whether lack of information is one reason politicians may be unresponsive to voter preferences using a randomized control trial in Pakistan.

“Can Party Elites Shape the Rank-and-File? Evidence from a Recruitment Experiment in India” (2025)
American Political Science Review. With Durgesh Pathak, Sarah Thompson, and Aliz Toth.
PDF Cite

We analyze a field experiment conducted by an Indian party that randomized recruitment messages. We show that elite efforts can shape the rank-and-file.

“Personalities and Public Sector Performance: Evidence from a Health Experiment in Pakistan” (2025)
Economic Development and Cultural Change. With Michael Callen, Ali Hasanain, et al.
PDF Cite

This paper provides evidence that the personalities of policymakers matter for policy outcomes in the health sector.

“Good Politicians: Experimental Evidence on Motivations for Political Candidacy” (2025)
Review of Economic Studies. With Muhammad Yasir Khan.
PDF Cite

How can we motivate good politicians to enter politics? We vary how political office is portrayed to ordinary citizens in a field experiment in Pakistan.

“Inaccurate Forecasting of a Randomized Controlled Trial” (2024)
Journal of Experimental Political Science. With Mats Ahrehshop, Miriam Golden, and Luke Sonnet.
PDF

We report results of a forecasting experiment about a randomized controlled trial that was conducted in the field.

“Extending the Formal State: The Case of Pakistan’s Frontier Crimes Regulation” (2024)
Economica. With Michael Callen, Arman Rezaee, and Jacob Shapiro.
PDF

Why do modern states allow parts of their territory to be governed by non-state actors? We explore this using the Frontier Crimes Regulation (FCR) in Pakistan.

“The Political Economy of Public Sector Absence” (2023)

PDF

“Can Campaign Contribution Limits Curb the Influence of Money in Politics?” (2022)
American Journal of Political Science. With Miguel R. Rueda and Nelson A. Ruiz.
PDF

Using a regression discontinuity design in Colombia, we show that looser limits increase the number of public contracts assigned to donors.

“How Campaigns Respond to Ballot Position: A New Mechanism for Order Effects” (2022)
Journal of Politics. With Thomas S. Robinson and Nelson A. Ruiz.
PDF

We show that campaigns react to being placed on top of the ballot by raising and spending more money.

“Who Enters Politics and Why?” (2021)

“Air pollution could be reduced by incentivizing local government officials to control crop burning“ (2025)
Nature: Policy Brief. With Gemma Dipoppa.
PDF Cite

Bureaucratic action to punish farmers deters future fires by a further 13%. Thus, using administrator incentives to control crop burning could guide policy.

“Bureaucrat Incentives Reduce Crop Burning and Child Mortality in South Asia“ (2024)
Nature. With Gemma Dipoppa.
PDF Cite

We show that crop burning responds to bureaucrats' incentives: fires increase by 15% when wind is most likely to direct pollution to neighboring jurisdictions.
